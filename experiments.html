Electrolyte Tester

What You’ll Learn

  • The concept of electrolytes and their role in conducting electricity.
  • How to differentiate between strong, weak, and non-electrolytes.
  • Basic circuit connections using an Arduino and OLED display.
  • How to visualize conductivity using digital output.

    • Materials Needed

    • Battery
    • Arduino board (Uno/Nano)
    • OLED display (0.96-inch recommended)
    • Conductivity probe or two metal electrodes (copper/stainless steel)
    • JUmper Wires
    • LED bulb
    • Various liquids (water, saltwater, juice)

    How to Do It

    1. Setup the Circuit:
      • Connect the electrodes to the Arduino’s analog input through a voltage divider using resistors.
      • Attach the OLED display to the Arduino using I2C connections (SDA to A4, SCL to A5 on an Uno)
      • Power the circuit using a USB or battery.
    2. Write the Code:
      • Program the Arduino to measure the voltage change when the electrodes are dipped in different solutions.
      • Display messages on the OLED screen indicating whether the solution is a strong electrolyte, weak electrolyte, or non-electrolyte.
      • Example: “Welcome Shreyash” scrolling until electrodes are dipped.
    3. Test with Solutions:
      • Dip the electrodes into different liquids and observe the readings on the OLED display.
      • Compare the conductivity levels for various substances.

    The Science Behind It

    • Electrolytes are substances that dissolve in water to produce ions, enabling electrical conductivity.
    • Strong electrolytes (e.g., saltwater) fully dissociate into ions and conduct well.
    • Weak electrolytes (e.g., vinegar) partially dissociate, leading to moderate conductivity.
    • Non-electrolytes (e.g., sugar water) do not produce ions, resulting in no or minimal conductivity.
    • The Arduino measures the voltage drop across the liquid, indicating its conductivity level, which is then displayed on the OLED screen.

    Particle Accelerator

    What You’ll Learn

    • How particle accelerators work (without actually breaking physics).
    • The magic of electromagnetic fields in moving tiny particles at high speeds.
    • Why scientists smash particles together and how it helps us understand the universe.
    • How to build a tiny accelerator (without causing a black hole in your house).

    Materials Needed

    • A strong neodymium magnet (but not so strong that it pulls the fridge across the room).
    • Copper wire (for science, not for selling as scrap!).
    • A high-voltage source (like a 9V battery—don’t worry, CERN won’t be calling).
    • A small metallic bead (your very own subatomic test subject!).
    • A plastic tube (to guide the particle so it doesn’t fly into your cat’s food bowl).
    • A switch (for dramatic effect when powering it on).

    How to Do It

    1. Create the Track:
      • Coil the copper wire around the plastic tube to make an electromagnetic loop.
      • Connect the wire to the battery using the switch to control the power.
    2. Launch the Particle:
      • Place the metallic bead inside the tube.
      • Activate the circuit to create an electromagnetic field, pushing the bead forward.
      • Watch in awe as your ‘particle’ speeds up (just not to light speed!).
    3. Observe the Madness:
      • Try using different voltages to see how speed is affected.
      • Add more loops and magnets to make your bead zoom faster.
      • Try launching it at targets (but not at your sibling!).

    The Science Behind It

    • Real particle accelerators (like CERN’s Large Hadron Collider) use massive magnets and electric fields to accelerate particles to near-light speeds.
    • Your mini-accelerator uses electromagnetic induction to push the metal bead forward—kind of like a maglev train but way cooler.
    • This is the same principle that powers futuristic railguns, MRI machines, and, well…science fiction dreams.
    • No, you won’t accidentally create a wormhole—but if your bead disappears, check under the couch.

    Newton’s Cradle

    What You’ll Learn

    • How momentum and energy transfer work in a perfect world (and why reality is slightly messier).
    • The magic of conservation laws (momentum and energy are like boomerangs—they always come back!).
    • Why Newton’s Cradle never gets tired, but you do.
    • How to build one and impress your friends with your newfound physics knowledge!

    Materials Needed

    • Five identical metal balls (not the ones from Grandpa’s antique clock!).
    • A sturdy frame (wood, plastic, or anything that won’t collapse in shame)
    • Strong string (because weak string = instant physics fail).

    How to Do It

    1. Build the Frame:
      • Construct a simple rectangular frame tall enough to let the balls swing freely.
    2. Attach the Balls:
      • Cut equal-length strings and attach them to the frame so that all balls hang in a perfect row.
      • Make sure they barely touch each other—just like polite people in an elevator.
    3. Start the Chain Reaction:
      • Pull one ball back and release it. Watch in amazement as the opposite ball shoots out like a physics magician’s trick!
      • Try pulling back two balls. Guess what? Two balls will move on the other side. (Newton knew his stuff!)

    The Science Behind It

  • Conservation of Momentum & Energy: The reason why the balls move so predictably is that energy and momentum transfer through the stationary balls almost perfectly.
  • Elastic Collisions: Since metal balls don’t absorb much energy, the force keeps bouncing back and forth like a stubborn argument.
  • Friction & Air Resistance: Sadly, in the real world, energy slowly gets lost due to air resistance and imperfect materials—so your cradle won’t swing forever (unless you have a frictionless universe in your garage!).

    Newton’s Disc

    What You’ll Learn

    • How white light is actually a mix of all colors (spoiler: rainbows aren’t just for aesthetics).
    • The science of persistence of vision—your eyes aren’t as fast as they think they are.
    • How to make colors disappear like a magic trick, but with actual physics.
    • Why Newton was not just about apples and laws of motion.

    Materials Needed

    • A sturdy cardboard or plastic disc (no, your mom’s kitchen plate won’t work).
    • White paper and colored markers or printed color segments.
    • A sharp compass or protractor (if you’re feeling fancy).
    • A straw, skewer, or motor (if you want to go pro mode).
    • Glue or tape (because chaos isn’t fun in DIY projects).

    How to Do It

    1. Create the Disc:
      • Cut out a circular disc from cardboard or plastic.
      • Divide it into seven equal sections and color each with a different rainbow color (VIBGYOR, or just Google it if that’s too much to remember).
      • Stick it onto a firm base so it doesn’t warp mid-spin (unless you want abstract art instead of physics).
    2. Make it Spin:
      • Pierce the center with a skewer/straw or attach it to a small motor if you like overkill.
      • Give it a spin fast enough, and—boom!—all the colors merge into white.
      • If it doesn’t work, spin it faster… or check if you swapped out a color for black by mistake.

    The Science Behind It

    • Your eyes don’t process images instantly—they lag slightly(known as persistence of vision(1/16th of a second)) (which is why fast-spinning things blur together instead of looking like a stop-motion disaster).
    • White light is made of all visible colors. When the disc spins fast, your brain can’t separate the individual colors, so they blend into white.
    • This is called color addition, the same principle that lets screens display every color using just red, green, and blue pixels.

    Lemon Battery

    What You’ll Learn

    • How to generate electricity using just a lemon (finally, fruit is useful for something other than juice!).
    • The science behind chemical reactions and how they create electrical energy.
    • Why metal combinations matter when making a battery.
    • That lemons have shocking potential (bad pun, but true).

    Materials Needed

    • A fresh lemon (or multiple, if you’re feeling ambitious).
    • A galvanized nail (zinc-coated, not the rusty one from your backyard).
    • A copper coin or strip (because pure copper > pocket change).
    • Alligator clips or wires (for making the magic happen).
    • A small LED or a multimeter (to check if your lemon power is legit).

    How to Do It

    1. Prepare the Lemon:
      • Roll it on a table with slight pressure to release more juice inside (don’t smash it—this isn’t a stress ball!).
      • Insert the zinc nail and the copper coin into the lemon, keeping them close but not touching (opposites attract, but not in chemistry!).
    2. Connect the Circuit:
      • Attach one alligator clip to the zinc nail and another to the copper coin.
      • Connect the free ends of the clips to an LED or multimeter.
      • Watch as your lemon battery lights up or gives a voltage reading—it’s alive (well, sort of)!
    3. Boost the Power:
      • Chain multiple lemons together by connecting copper from one to zinc of another.
      • Try powering a small digital clock or buzzer. Congrats, you just made a citrus-powered gadget!

    The Science Behind It

    • The lemon juice acts as an electrolyte, allowing ions to flow and create an electric current.
    • Zinc is more reactive than copper, so electrons move from zinc to copper, generating voltage.
    • This is the same principle behind real batteries—except you won’t find “lemon-powered” gadgets in stores (yet!).

    Water Fireworks

    What You’ll Learn

    • How liquids of different densities interact (it’s like oil and water, but way cooler).
    • The science of diffusion and why colors swirl like mini fireworks.
    • Why dish soap is more than just for cleaning dirty plates.
    • How to make a chemical-free fireworks show right at home.

    Materials Needed

    • A clear glass or bowl filled with water (because murky water = sad science).
    • Vegetable oil (because fire-free fireworks are the goal).
    • Food coloring (go wild with colors!).
    • A fork (for science-y mixing, not eating).

    How to Do It

    1. Prepare the Firework Mix:
      • Pour a few tablespoons of vegetable oil into a separate small bowl.
      • Add several drops of different food coloring to the oil.
      • Use a fork to gently mix the colors into tiny droplets (but don’t blend it into a mess!).
    2. Create the Show:
      • Pour the oil-and-color mixture into the clear water.
      • Watch as the colors slowly sink and burst into mesmerizing patterns—like fireworks in slow motion!
    3. Experiment with Effects:
      • Try using warm or cold water and see how it changes the diffusion speed.
      • Stir the water gently to create swirling effects.
      • Add a drop of dish soap and watch the colors spread even faster!

    The Science Behind It

    • Oil and water don’t mix: Oil floats on top because it’s less dense than water.
    • Food coloring is water-based: When it sinks through the oil, it bursts into the water like tiny firework explosions.
    • Diffusion at work: The color spreads naturally through the water, creating the stunning effect.

    Homemade pH Indicator

    What You’ll Learn

    • How to test acids and bases using a natural indicator (because chemicals are so last season).
    • Why red cabbage is basically a built-in chemistry lab.
    • The science behind pH levels and color changes.
    • How to turn your kitchen into a mad scientist’s lab (minus the explosions).

    Materials Needed

    • A few leaves of red cabbage (not green, unless you want disappointment).
    • Hot water (for extracting the magical color-changing juice).
    • A blender or knife (to chop the cabbage—no, we’re not making salad).
    • Coffee filter or strainer (to separate the indicator juice).
    • Clear cups or test tubes (the fancier, the better!).
    • Household substances for testing: vinegar, lemon juice, baking soda, soap, soda, etc.

    How to Do It

    1. Extract the pH Indicator:
      • Chop the red cabbage into small pieces.
      • Pour hot water over the cabbage and let it sit for 10-15 minutes.
      • Strain the liquid into a cup—it should be a deep purple color. Congrats, you just made a natural pH indicator!
    2. Test the pH:
      • Pour a little of your cabbage juice into separate clear cups.
      • Add a different household substance to each cup.
      • Observe the color changes like a true scientist!
    3. What the Colors Mean:
      • Red/Pink = Acidic (Lemon juice, vinegar, soda).
      • Purple = Neutral (Water, because it’s Switzerland in chemistry).
      • Green/Blue = Basic (Baking soda, soap, detergent).

    The Science Behind It

    • Red cabbage contains anthocyanins, natural pigments that change color depending on the pH.
    • In acidic solutions, they turn reddish-pink, in neutral solutions they stay purple, and in basic solutions, they shift to blue-green.
    • It’s the same reason hydrangea flowers change color based on soil pH!

    Magic Milk

    What You’ll Learn

    • How surface tension works (and how to break it like a science rebel).
    • Why soap is more than just for washing hands.
    • How to create a swirling rainbow with just milk and colors.
    • That playing with food can be educational (sometimes)

    Materials Needed

    • A shallow dish or plate (because deep bowls are party poopers here).
    • Whole milk (not water, not skim—go full fat for full effect!).
    • Food coloring (more colors = more awesomeness).
    • Dish soap (the hero of this experiment).
    • Cotton swabs (because fingers are messy but tempting).

    How to Do It

    1. Prepare the Canvas:
      • Pour enough milk into the dish to cover the bottom.
      • Add drops of different food coloring near the center—don’t stir yet, we’re building suspense!
    2. Trigger the Magic:
      • Dip a cotton swab into dish soap.
      • Lightly touch the surface of the milk with the soapy swab.
      • Watch in amazement as the colors shoot out and swirl around like an art masterpiece in motion!
    3. Experiment with Variations:
      • Try different types of milk (skim, almond, heavy cream) and see which works best.
      • Use different amounts of soap to test how much is needed for an explosion of color.
      • Try dipping multiple soapy swabs at once—because science deserves extra chaos!

    The Science Behind It

    • Milk contains fats and proteins, which are affected by dish soap.
    • Soap breaks surface tension and chases the fat molecules around, dragging the food coloring along for the ride.
    • The swirling effect happens because soap is spreading out, dissolving fat, and pushing colors in all directions.

    Hydrogen Balloon Explosion

    What You’ll Learn

    • How to make hydrogen gas from just water (yes, really!).
    • The basics of electrolysis and why water isn’t just H2O in disguise.
    • How hydrogen is lighter than air (hello, science party trick!).
    • Why hydrogen is used in rockets—and why you should be careful.

    Materials Needed

    • A glass or plastic container filled with water (tap water works, but distilled is top-tier).
    • Two graphite rods (from pencil leads, not your dad’s expensive pen).
    • A 9V battery and battery clips (power = progress!).
    • Wires with alligator clips (for easy connections).
    • A balloon (for capturing the magic gas).
    • Dish soap (to make bubbles, because science should be fun).
    • A long-handled lighter or candle (for the grand finale!).

    How to Do It

    1. Set Up Electrolysis:
      • Fill the container with water and add a pinch of salt or baking soda (to improve conductivity).
      • Attach one graphite rod to the positive terminal (anode) and the other to the negative terminal (cathode) of the battery using alligator clips.
      • Place both rods in the water without touching.
      • Watch as bubbles start forming—oxygen at the anode, hydrogen at the cathode (spoiler: we want the hydrogen!).
    2. Collect the Hydrogen:
      • Hold the opening of a balloon over the hydrogen-producing electrode.
      • Allow the balloon to fill up (patience, young scientists!).
      • Tie off the balloon carefully—you’ve just captured one of the most explosive gases known to science!
    3. Time for the Boom! (Safety First!)!
      • Go to an open, safe outdoor area (seriously, don’t do this in your room!).
      • Use a long lighter to bring a flame close to the balloon.
      • Stand back and prepare for a loud pop with a mini fireball—congrats, you just created a hydrogen explosion!

    The Science Behind It

    • Electrolysis splits H2O into hydrogen (H2) and oxygen (O2) gases using electric current.
    • Hydrogen is highly flammable, meaning it reacts explosively with oxygen when ignited.
    • This is the same principle used in rocket fuel—so technically, you just made a (tiny) rocket explosion!
